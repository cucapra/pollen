\section{Overview}

In the field of computational biology, there is much excitement about the
\emph{pangenome}. The traditional approach in genomics has long been to
compare a given sample to a single reference genome, but this has a
severe drawback: it is inherently overfitted to the reference, and such overfitting is known to have devastating consequences.
In contrast, the pangenome facilitates a fine-grained many-to-many comparison of samples,
thus allowing a much richer exploration of the similarities and differences
between the individuals of a species.

Traditional genomic sequencing is famously expensive in terms of time and money.
Constructing a pangenome, which is typically represented as a mathematical graph, is even more so.
We do not wish to create a health
solution that is open exclusively to the rich and powerful,
so the \emph{efficient} construction of
pangenomes is an area of active development.

Once constructed, a pangenome can be queried to yield results that help
identify aberrations in the human genome that may be the sources of diseases and
genetic proclivities. Used at its full potential, this technology could
radically change medicial diagnoses and treatment. This has a
direct tie to Sustainable Development Goal 3, ``Good Health and Well-Being''
\cite{sdg}.

To step back briefly, the broader field of genomics is itself in an exciting moment: after decades of research, researchers recently published the first \emph{complete} sequence of a human genome.
Their work is explained over six papers in a special issue of \emph{Science}~\cite{science}.

In an annotated bibliography, submitted previously, we survey a few existing methods for the construction of pangenome graphs.
For our final project, we propose to \emph{compare} these methods, getting to the bottom of what the different approaches to graph construction buy us.

\section{Summary of annotated bibliography}

Our prior survey showed that there are, broadly, two techniques for the construction of pangenome graphs: multiple sequence alignment~\cite{poa} and
the reduction of alignments to a sequence graph~\cite{genome_alignment}.
The industry standard is a model called the GFA.

We studied two projects that are in active development in this area: Minigraph~\cite{minigraph} is concerned with maintaining the original coordinate system even after pangenomic alignment, and Progressive Cactus~\cite{genome_alignment} uses a novel method to break down the problem and thus enjoys gains in performance.

We looked into Odgi~\cite{odgi}, which takes an opinionated step away from the above efforts in an attempt at more compact representations, especially when \emph{loops} are possible.

We studied two projects that provide a ``complete package'' solution.
PPanGGolin~\cite{pangolin} is the tool of choice for studying bacterial pangenomes, and panX~\cite{panx} contributes a web-based GUI that visualizes pangenomes.

Finally, we wondered if it was possible to take a different approach altogether, allowing a framework such as CLR-DRNets~\cite{clrdrnets}
to tackle the problem in an unsupervised or weakly-supervised setting while allowing it to lean on natural patterns and rules that dictate how genomes appear in nature.

\section{The Project}

For our project, we propose to engage in a deeper analysis still, critically comparing the approaches discussed above.
What follows is a taste of the kinds of questions we will try to answer.

What does the choice of construction method buy us?
Has further work shown any one method to be superior, or is this just a stylistic choice?
Are the methods fundamentally incompatible, or can there be a sort of \emph{compiler} that automatically maps a query written against one flavor of pangenome graph to a (series of) queries written against the other flavor of graph?

When it comes to the bleeding edge, the questions remain similar.
Is there anything fundamentally preventing us from getting the best of both worlds, i.e., Minigraph's stable coordinate system along with Progressive Cactus's performance gains?
Further, are there other projects at the bleeding edge that we have neglected to mention?

Odgi takes a step away from the GFA standard in order to realize its compact representation.
It maintains backward compatability with GFAs, but this converstion is a heavyweight operation.
What exactly was the fundamental limitation in the GFA model?
Could a form of ``decorated'' GFA have done the trick, allowing Odgi's wins to be realized in the standard format?

What factor about PPanGGolin limits it to bacterial study?
Are these ``just'' scaling issues, or does the tool bake something bacteria-specific deep into its design? Do the visualizations created by panX have true utility in the realm of genomics, or are they more suited for the purposes of pedagogy and popular science?

Lastly, how can CLR-DRNets be instantiated towards pangenome construction?
What ``rules'' about the genome are useful to the tool, and what trends are harder to take advantage of?

\section{Related Work}

A variety of other projects~\cite{sailfish, alexandrov,omichessan} have also embarked on surveys of genomic methods, though not always involving the pangenome. We will also comment briefly on their work and try to point out any fundamental incompatibilites with the pangenome.
